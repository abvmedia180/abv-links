The Problem

Charitable giving has a trust problem.

Every year, hundreds of billions of dollars move from donors to charitable organizations. A meaningful share of it does not reach the people it was meant to serve. For donors who pay attention, this is not a secret. It is the reason the sector has a credibility crisis.

The Origin

Seven years of watching. Fifteen days to act.

Kind Steward was not a plan. It was a reaction.

For years, Abe Varughese watched close family members — committed, faithful, tithing Christians — write substantial checks to charitable organizations, and he watched, too often, those dollars disappear into waste, overhead, politics, and "mission trips" that looked more like vacations than mission work. The running question became sharper every year: what are they actually doing with the money?

The thesis that eventually became Kind Steward emerged from that observation. A single year of a family's tithe check, handed to a trusted-but-unaccountable intermediary, doesn't move the needle. But three or four years of the same giving, pooled and deployed directly — food on a street in New York, a corrective surgery for a child in Ethiopia, a winter coat in Texas — can change lives measurably. The gap between those two outcomes is not charity. It is discipline.

In mid-2025, a local institutional-trust failure in a nonprofit Abe's family had supported crystallized what had been a seven-year concern. The exact incident is not the point. The point is that the people running that organization were not held to account, and the money that should have gone to the mission went elsewhere. Abe's family had written checks for more than one person's car. The beneficiaries of those checks were not the people the organization claimed to serve.

That was the end of the watching.

"If you don't like the way something is done, go out and do it yourself."

The Reluctant Founder

Nobody wanted to start a charity.

This is important context: Kind Steward is not a vanity project, a resume line, or an empire-building exercise. Nobody on the board set out to build a nonprofit. They all have careers, companies, families, and ten other things going at full speed.

Kind Steward exists because, after enough years of looking, the founder could not find a charitable organization he trusted enough to hand his family's money to. The gap was real. If nothing else filled it, the only remaining option was to build one that would.

The plain, cold version:

"There's nobody I trust to give money to where I can be confident it will go to the right resources and actually impact people. There is so much corruption, so many people taking money. That's it. That's the whole reason."

From decision to filing took fifteen days. The Kind Steward Foundation was incorporated in Wyoming on July 29, 2025.

One mission. Two entities.

They share a board, a mission, and a registered office — but they play different roles in the model, because private and public charitable money follow different rules. Here's how they fit together.

Why Two

Each structure unlocks something the other can't.

A private foundation can be founder-controlled, family-governed, and funded from within. It can be stood up in fifteen days with a small, aligned group, and without a public fundraising plan. It can hold and deploy family assets, take appreciated securities, and operate on a five-year horizon without needing to persuade the public of anything. The Gates, Bezos, Buffett, and Rockefeller organizations all use this structure for a reason: it was purpose-built for this use case.

A public charity is the only vehicle that can cleanly accept donations from the public, run a "donate" button without legal gray zones, qualify for the $120,000/year Google Ad Grant, and unlock the nonprofit discount ecosystem from Microsoft, Google, QuickBooks, and the rest of the tech stack. It also offers higher donor-deductibility caps (60% of AGI for cash gifts, versus 30% for private foundation gifts). In exchange, it accepts public-support obligations, higher IRS scrutiny, and the annual discipline of publishing its numbers.

Every serious philanthropic operation has this split. It is not a hack, a loophole, or a workaround. It is standard practice for founders and families who take charitable work seriously. Kind Steward does what they do, for the same reason: each structure lets the other focus on what it does best.

The practical answer Large gifts, appreciated assets, or founder-level contributions → the Foundation. Small-to-mid cash donations, monthly support, or peer-driven giving → the Charity. If you don't know which, give to the Charity. Your dollar lands the same place.

The Commitment

Every public dollar reaches a person.

100%

To Program

The Foundation absorbs all overhead — formation, registered agent, accounting, banking, legal, compliance, software, infrastructure. The Charity operates as a pure pass-through on program dollars. Every donation made by a member of the public is dedicated to programs: the surgery, the brace, the meal, the jacket, the well. Transaction fees are absorbed or opted-in by the donor, not deducted from the program dollar.

This is not a fundraising slogan. It is the structural purpose of having a separately-funded Foundation in the first place. When the two vehicles are wired this way, donor dollars and operational dollars come from different places by design. Most charities cannot credibly make this promise. Kind Steward's architecture was built to.

The Precedent

This isn't a new idea.

The 100% pass-through architecture is not something Kind Steward invented. It was pioneered by charity: water, founded in 2006 by Scott Harrison after a similar observation about how donor money gets absorbed by the organizations it passes through.

charity: water runs on two separate accounts: one for public donations, 100% of which fund clean water projects, and a second "operating" account funded by a closed circle of entrepreneurs and families who understand that running a charity has real costs — and who prefer to pay those costs themselves, so that every public dollar can go directly to the work.

It works. charity: water has raised hundreds of millions of dollars on this model, and the structure is now taught as a case study at Harvard Business School and the Stanford Graduate School of Business.

charity: water · 2006

Two separate accounts. One rule. Every public dollar goes to the water project.

Kind Steward applies the same architecture, tuned for a two-entity legal structure — a private foundation covering overhead, a public charity delivering the programs. Different vehicles, same discipline: donor money and operational money do not mix.

Operating Model

Run it like an investment fund for impact.

Charities are usually run on mission statements and best intentions. Budgets get built around the organization rather than around the people the organization serves. The gap is where the bloat lives.

"My goal is to run this like an investment fund. I want to see what is the maximum impact we can have per dollar. I hate that charities have so much bloat. I just don't want to see money wasted." — Founder

Kind Steward's operating discipline is to refuse the bloat and measure every decision against a single question: what is the maximum human impact this dollar can produce?

Two delivery channels follow from that discipline:

Direct to the source. Get the dollar to the person in need with the shortest possible chain of intermediaries. Homeless people on the streets of New York, Dallas, and Florida. Families who need food, shelter, clothing, medical care.

Partner with trusted operators already on the ground. People who are doing the work, know the community, and have the relationships — but who are operating at personal scale out of their own pocket. Kind Steward's role is to supply the capital and the accountability framework so their work can scale.

Both channels share the same discipline: documentation for every dollar. At Kind Steward, documentation is not paperwork. It is the product.

What Makes Us Different

Six structural commitments, not six slogans.

Most of what separates Kind Steward from the nonprofits that lost our trust is built into the governing documents, not written in the mission statement. These are the six commitments that are load-bearing, and why each one matters.

01

Business operators, not career nonprofit staff.

The team's training is in running businesses, not running organizations. Operating discipline, common sense, and outcomes orientation applied to a sector that often lacks all three.

02

100% pass-through on public donations.

All overhead, compliance, legal, accounting, and operating costs are absorbed by the Foundation. Every dollar a public donor gives goes to program. Structural, not aspirational.

03

Documentation is the product.

Every program traces dollar-to-beneficiary. Every trip produces photographic and video evidence. Every dollar is on the books, auditable. This is not an add-on; it's the deliverable.

04

Show the work. Don't beg for the money.

No guilt campaigns, no sob-story emails, no donor pressure. Do the work, document it, put it in front of the public via the Google Ad Grant, and let people decide. The work is the ask.

05

Zero extraction, by design.

No director, no officer, no founder takes a salary. Reimbursement for documented expenses only. Written into both entities' bylaws. When nobody is getting paid, nobody can turn the organization into a vehicle for personal enrichment.

06

An annual letter — signed, numeric, honest.

A Buffett-style founder's letter, every year. How many surgeries funded, meals sponsored, jackets distributed, dollars deployed, dollars documented. The entire organization compressed into ten minutes of honest math.

The Plan

Start narrow. Prove it. Earn the next move.

Kind Steward is built around disciplined emergence, not a rigid five-year plan. We're not going to pretend we know what the right Year 3 program is on Day 1. We're going to execute the first program with business-operator discipline, document the impact, and let the work attract the next donors and the next opportunities.

Phase 1 · October 2026

Ethiopia + Kenya pilot — pediatric corrective surgery

Abe and Kevin travel with the on-the-ground partners who have been personally funding these surgeries out of their own pockets for years. We're not starting from scratch. We are formalizing, verifying, and scaling a program that already works under trusted operators. The trip's purpose is verification — every step of the dollar-to-beneficiary chain.

Phase 2 · In Parallel

Domestic direct aid — NY, FL, TX

Sponsor direct-feeding operations for the homeless (including a verified weekly volunteer run near Penn Station). Winter essentials. Direct medical. All through proven local operators who have already done the work.

Phase 3 · Emergence

Wells. Housing. Orphanages. Schools.

Each new program is earned by proving the previous phase worked. The specific roadmap is deliberately open. The October trip opens doors. The doors dictate the next move.

Governance

Small, fast, trusted, unpaid.

The pattern that consistently separates organizations that execute from organizations that stall is simple: who's in the room. Kind Steward's governance was designed around that observation, and every clause in the bylaws reflects it.

0

compensation taken by any director or officer. Written into both entities' bylaws. Reimbursement for documented expenses only.

$10K

threshold above which disbursements require dual written approval from the President and Treasurer. Institutional check on every large outflow.

3/4

supermajority required to amend the bylaws or remove a director, with the President's affirmative vote required for amendments.

The governance design is explicit about three non-negotiables: speed (decisions don't get parked in committee), trust (the board is a working team, not a collection of stakeholders), and zero extraction (nobody is getting paid, so nobody is incentivized to game the organization). It's the inverse of what made the origin-story institutions fail.

The Annual Letter

The numbers, on the record, every year.

Kind Steward will publish an annual letter to its donors and the public. Not glossy. Not vague. Not a stock-photo "impact report." A founder's letter in the tradition of Warren Buffett's shareholder letters — signed, specific, numeric, and honest.

"This year we funded 300 corrective surgeries. We sponsored 2,000 meals. We distributed 500 jackets. Here is where every dollar went, and here is who received it." — The format of what we'll report

The format is the promise. Specific outcomes. Specific numbers. Specific dollars. A direct tie from donor inputs to beneficiary outcomes. The entire organization's year of work compressed into a document a donor can read in ten minutes and come away knowing exactly what their contribution did.

The institutions Kind Steward was built in response to couldn't write a letter like this. The numbers wouldn't survive public scrutiny. We write one by design, every year. The first letter publishes after the October 2026 trip.
