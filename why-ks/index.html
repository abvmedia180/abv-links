The Public Support Test · Defined

What "public charity" actually requires.

To keep public charity status, at least one-third of our total support must come from public sources — measured over a rolling five-year window.

Who counts as "public"
The general public, government units, other public charities.
Single-donor limit
Any one donor's gift counts toward public support only up to 2% of total support.
Fallback
If we miss the 1/3 test, we can qualify at 10% under facts-and-circumstances.

In plain English: the Charity can't be quietly funded by the Foundation and a few big donors. It has to attract genuine public support. That's what keeps us accountable — and it's why the Google Ad Grant and public fundraising aren't optional.

The Precedent

This isn't a new idea.

We're following a road some great organizations have already built — and applying the lessons across more programs than any one of them does.

charity: water · 2006

The 100% Model.

Two separate accounts: public donations fund water projects; private donors cover overhead. Hundreds of millions raised. Taught at Harvard and Stanford.

Smile Train · 1999

Unit economics for pediatric surgery.

~$250 per cleft palate surgery. Local surgeons, local hospitals, direct-to-child model. 1.5 million+ surgeries funded globally.

Against Malaria Foundation · 2004

Radical transparency. Per-unit impact.

~$5 per mosquito net. Every net tracked to destination. GiveWell-rated as one of the most cost-effective charities in the world.

GiveDirectly · 2009

Direct to beneficiary. No intermediaries.

Cash transfers directly to the world's poorest households. Outcomes tracked per household. Inspired a global rethink of how aid is delivered.

We're not inventing this. We're applying it — across pediatric surgery, homeless care, water, food, and shelter.
