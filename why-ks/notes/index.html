Part 1 · My Story

Why I started Kind Steward.

I want to tell you the real version of how this started. Not the website version. The real one.

For about seven or eight years, I watched my sister Amanda and her husband Abee, who are devout Christian tithers, write substantial checks to churches and nonprofit organizations. Every year. Faithfully. Their theology is straightforward — "I'm giving my 10% to the Lord. I trust the organization to do what they're going to do with it."

And every year I'd ask myself the question nobody in the community seemed to want to ask: what are they actually doing with it?

Because what I kept seeing — in the Malayali Christian community, in other nonprofit settings, in the broader sector — was waste. Real, visible waste. Donations getting absorbed into bloated overhead. Mission trips that were essentially vacations with a devotional veneer. Programs that produced no measurable outcome. And, in more than a few cases, active corruption.

I never did anything about it for years. I didn't have the legal knowledge, the time, or the infrastructure. But the thesis was forming: if you pooled three or four years of one family's tithe and went direct to people — a homeless person on the street in New York, a child who needs a surgery in Ethiopia — you could change lives. Real lives. Measurably. The gap between that and writing another check to another intermediary is enormous.

The tipping point — 2025.

In March 2025, a local nonprofit my family had supported was raising money to buy a building. I had serious concerns about the deal. I had been hearing from people close to the transaction that there were conflicts of interest, self-dealing, allegedly insider payments to people involved in structuring it — overinflated purchase prices, the whole picture. I don't want to name the organization, but the pattern was clear enough that I wrote a formal escalation letter. First to the youth pastor. Then it went up to the main pastor. I was explicit: "If you move forward with this as I'm hearing it's structured, you are breaking the law. It's a felony. You are the leader of this church, and I'm escalating this to you."

I'm not a member of that church. I was trying to protect my family's money and trying to protect the institution from itself. I was, predictably, treated as the problem. The broader Indian Christian community reaction was "don't rock the boat." The deal closed anyway, over Memorial Day weekend. And in early July 2025, I got confirmation from someone close to the transaction that the allegations I had raised were accurate.

That was the moment.

"If you don't like the way something is done, go out and do it yourself."

About fifteen days later, I had chosen a structure. On July 29, 2025, Kind Steward Foundation was filed in Wyoming.

Why I didn't want to do this.

I want to be honest about this too, because the audience should hear it. I did not want to start a charity. I'd never imagined myself starting a charity. I have a dozen other things going on in my life — businesses, investments, family. Running a nonprofit was never on my list.

What tipped me over the edge wasn't ambition. It was the absence of a trusted alternative. I could not find a charitable organization I trusted enough to hand my family's money to. And if the ones that exist aren't doing it right, the only remaining option is to build one that will.

I think that's important context when you hear the rest of this. Kind Steward isn't a vanity project. It's a reaction. It exists because the alternative was unacceptable.

Why the name.

The name is a thesis, not a brand exercise. Kind is the posture — mercy, proximity, warmth toward the people we serve. Steward is the operational and theological core. A steward is someone entrusted with resources that belong to someone else, and whose job is to deploy those resources faithfully, transparently, and with accountability. In the Christian frame my family operates within, the money isn't ours — it's the Lord's. Kind Steward exists to be a trustworthy steward of it: to close the gap between the giver's intention and the recipient's benefit.

The organizations that lost my trust weren't bad stewards by accident. They were bad stewards because no one held them to account. Kind Steward is designed to be the accountability.

Part 2 · Slide-by-Slide

What to say on each slide.

Short talking points for each of the 23 slides. Use as prompts, not scripts. The slides are designed so you narrate; they anchor.

Slide 01 The Why · Cover

Shows: Title card. Logo. "The Why."

Open simply. "Thanks for coming. I want to walk you through why we started Kind Steward, how it's structured, and what we're going to do with it. Let's call this the why."

Don't over-introduce. The title does the work.

Slide 02 The Question

Shows: "Where does the money actually go?"

This is the problem you want to plant. "Every year, hundreds of billions of dollars move from donors to charitable organizations. A real portion of that money doesn't reach the people it was meant to serve. And most donors have no way to find out."

Let it land.

Slide 03 70%

Shows: Giant 70% stat. Green background.

Pause on this number. "70% of Americans say they don't trust how charitable organizations use the money they raise. Most donors have been burned. Most donors are right to be skeptical. The trust gap in this sector is measurable and massive."

Slide 04 What We Kept Seeing

Shows: Chip row — "Overhead · Mission-trip vacations · Self-dealing · Insider contracts · Unclear impact · Zero accountability"

"The pattern is the same everywhere. Donor dollars get absorbed — into overhead, into 'mission trips' that are really vacations, into insider contracts, into vague 'impact' claims nobody can verify. For years, I watched my family's own tithe dollars go into this pattern. And I couldn't unsee it."

This is where you can add one specific example from your story — without naming organizations — if the room is right for it.

Slide 05 The Shift

Shows: "If nobody will do it right, do it yourself."

This is your origin moment. Say it in your voice. "In the middle of 2025, after another round of watching the same pattern play out with institutions my family had supported, I decided I was done watching. If nobody was going to do it right, I'd do it myself. That's the entire reason this organization exists."

Slide 06 15 Days

Shows: 15 days · Kind Steward Foundation filed July 29, 2025.

"Fifteen days from decision to filing. On July 29, 2025, the Kind Steward Foundation was incorporated in Wyoming as a 501(c)(3) private foundation. No committee. No five-year planning process. A clear decision and then action."

Slide 07 Two Entities. One Mission.

Shows: Hero-scale title. Green.

This sets up the structural explanation. "Now, the structure. We have two separate entities, not one. The Kind Steward Foundation and Kind Steward. Same mission, but they play different roles, because private charitable money and public charitable money follow completely different rules. Let me walk you through how they fit together."

Slide 08 The Diagram

Shows: Full entity flow diagram — Donors, Foundation, Charity, Programs.

Walk through the diagram in order.

Top row, green. "Private donors — the board, our families, and close friends — fund the Foundation. The Foundation holds those assets, covers all of our overhead, and makes grants to other 501(c)(3) organizations."

Bottom row, amber. "Public donors fund the Charity. Anyone in the public, any size donation, through the website or the Google Ad Grant inbound. The Charity runs the actual programs on the ground — surgery, food, shelter, care."

The dashed arrows in the middle. "The Foundation can grant money to the Charity — and it will, as part of its required 5% annual distribution. The Foundation also covers the Charity's overhead. That's why every public donation can go 100% to program."

Slide 09 Foundation Deep Dive

Shows: Green slide. Foundation details — EIN, tax status, directors, role.

"Kind Steward Foundation. A 501(c)(3) private foundation under §509(a). Formed July 2025 in Wyoming. Four directors — closely aligned, which private foundations are allowed to have. Public charities face stricter independent-director scrutiny; private foundations don't. That's part of why this was our Stage 1 vehicle. Funded from within. Required to distribute at least 5% of its assets for charitable purposes every year."

Short highlight: "Think of the Foundation as the private capital engine. It holds the money. It covers our overhead. And it makes grants."

Slide 10 Charity Deep Dive

Shows: Cream slide. Charity details — EIN, tax status, directors, role.

"Kind Steward — the Charity. A 501(c)(3) public charity under §509(a)(1). Formed April 2026 in Wyoming. Five directors — the same four plus Katherine Daniel, who is our Director of Communications and who is independent of the family. That independent director matters to the IRS."

"Funded by the public. Required to pass the public support test — at least one-third of its support has to come from public sources. Think of the Charity as the public operating engine. It runs the programs. It receives donations. It delivers the aid."

Slide 11 The Public Support Test

Shows: Warm-bg slide. Plain-English definition of the Public Support Test with a 3-column breakdown.

Slow down here because people often ask about it. "To keep our public charity status with the IRS, we have to prove that at least one third of our total support comes from public sources. Measured over a rolling five-year window."

Walk the 3 columns: "Public means the general public, government units, or other public charities. Any single donor's gift only counts toward public support up to 2% of total — so we can't just take a couple of big checks and call ourselves publicly supported. The IRS designed this rule specifically to force genuine public fundraising. If we don't hit the 1/3 test, there's a 10% fallback under a facts-and-circumstances analysis."

Landing: "Practically, what this means is the Foundation can't be the Charity's only funder. Public donations aren't optional. That's why the Google Ad Grant is so important — it's the engine that keeps us publicly supported at the 5-year review."

Slide 12 The Promise — Headline

Shows: Black slide. "Every public dollar reaches a person."

Slow down here. "This is the commitment we built the entire structure around. Every public dollar reaches a person. Not 85%, not 92%. 100%."

Slide 13 100%

Shows: Giant 100% with concentric rings. Deep green.

"Here's how it works. The Foundation absorbs all of our overhead — the legal fees, the accounting, the banking, the compliance, the software, the formation costs, all of it. The family funds the Foundation specifically so that overhead never touches donor money. The Charity then operates as a pure pass-through on every public dollar it receives. You give $100, $100 goes to the surgery, the meal, the jacket."

"This isn't a fundraising slogan. It's the structural reason we have two entities in the first place. Most charities can't make this promise because their operating costs have to come out of somewhere, and that somewhere is almost always donor money. Ours doesn't."

Slide 14 Proven Models — 4 precedents

Shows: 2x2 grid of precedent cards — charity: water, Smile Train, Against Malaria Foundation, GiveDirectly.

Don't read all four. Hit the headline and then walk through one or two. "If this sounds too good to be true, it's not. We're following a road that some great organizations have already built."

Then pick whichever lands for the audience: "charity: water, founded by Scott Harrison in 2006, runs on exactly the 100% model I just described. Two accounts. Public dollars fund the water projects. Private donors cover overhead. Hundreds of millions raised. Now taught at Harvard and Stanford."

For pediatric surgery in particular: "Smile Train has funded over 1.5 million cleft palate surgeries globally at about $250 apiece. That's the unit-economics model we're replicating with our Ethiopia pediatric work."

Close: "We're not inventing this. We're applying it — across more programs than any one of them does."

Slide 15 Six Commitments

Shows: 6-card pillar grid.

Don't read all six. Hit the top three: "Business operators running a charity — we come from running businesses, not running nonprofits. 100% pass-through — which I just walked you through. And zero extraction — no one on the board, including me, takes a salary. Ever. It's written into both bylaws."

"The rest you can read. The point is these aren't slogans. They're structural choices in the governing documents."

Slide 16 Impact Per Dollar

Shows: Bar chart — meal, jacket, brace, surgery, Manhattan dinner.

This is the emotional/rational climax for donors. "Let me show you what one donation actually does. $8 funds a hot meal through a trusted volunteer group. $40 funds a winter jacket for someone on a New York street. $50 funds a brace for a child. $70 to $300 funds a corrective surgery for a child in Ethiopia that their family could not otherwise afford."

Pause on the anchor. "For reference — a dinner for two at a Manhattan restaurant runs about $200. That same $200 can fund multiple surgeries, feed dozens of people, or buy winter coats for five. That is what 'impact per dollar' actually looks like when you go direct to the source."

Slide 17 At Scale — bigger programs

Shows: Warm-bg slide with 7 program cards — mosquito nets, school supplies, cleft surgery, orphan sponsorship, school year, wells, schools/orphanages.

This is where you expand the vision. "That last slide was what one individual donation does. But when donations add up, the scale of what becomes possible changes completely."

Walk them top-to-bottom. Don't read every card — hit the anchors. "Five dollars funds a mosquito net. Twenty-five funds school supplies for a child. Two-fifty funds a cleft palate surgery, which is the Smile Train model. Fifty a month sponsors an orphan — that's the Compassion International model. Four hundred covers a full year of school for a child in East Africa. Seven thousand drills a clean-water well that serves 500 to 1,000 people for 10 to 20 years. And twenty-five to fifty thousand a year covers a small village school or an orphanage home."

The takeaway: "This is the full range of what Kind Steward can fund over time. We're starting with pediatric surgery and homeless care because those are proven, but the structure we've built scales across all of it."

Slide 18 The Plan — Header

Shows: "Start narrow. Prove it. Earn the next move."

"Here's how we actually execute. We are not pretending to know what the right Year 3 program is on Day 1. We're going to start narrow, prove it, document it honestly, and earn the next move. Disciplined emergence, not a master plan."

Part 4 · Q&A Prep

Questions you might actually get.

"How is 100% pass-through really possible?"

The Foundation absorbs all operating costs — legal, compliance, accounting, banking, software, compliance. It's separately funded by the founding family specifically for that purpose. The Charity runs a pure program-only budget, which is why every public donation can go to program. Transaction fees (credit card, bank) are absorbed by the Foundation or opted into by the donor on the donation form. charity: water has been running this exact structure since 2006.

"Isn't having close relationships on the board a red flag?"

Not for a private foundation — that structure expressly allows a closely-aligned board and is the standard vehicle founders and families use for exactly this reason. For the Charity (public charity) we added Katherine Daniel as an independent fifth director, and the bylaws require two disinterested directors to approve any related-party transaction or compensation decision. The IRS looks for that exact governance structure on public charities.

"Why Wyoming?"

Standard choice for serious holding entities and foundations. Lowest admin friction, cheapest compliance, no annual state report for nonprofits. Same state many well-known foundations are domiciled in. No gimmick — just the better jurisdiction.

"When can I donate?"

The Charity's Form 1023 is being prepared. Once the IRS approves tax-exempt status, we'll stand up the donation platform. That should be late 2026 / early 2027. For now, reach out at info@KindSteward.org if you want to be on the launch list.

"What if the October trip doesn't produce the expected unit economics?"

Then we update the numbers and report them honestly. Every annual letter publishes the real figures, not the projected ones. That's the point.

"What about accountability when things go wrong?"

Three structural safeguards. Dual approval over $10,000. No compensation, so no one has a personal incentive to protect bad work. Annual letter with numeric reporting that invites public scrutiny. If something's off, it shows up in the numbers.

"How is this different from just giving to charity: water or similar organizations?"

charity: water does one thing: water. We cover multiple programs that the same donor dollar wouldn't otherwise reach — pediatric surgery in Ethiopia, domestic homeless services in NY/FL/TX, winter essentials, food. And we're choosing programs where the unit economics are extraordinary and verifiable. Complementary, not competitive.
